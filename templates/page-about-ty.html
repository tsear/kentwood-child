Project Management Approach

Execution starts with clarity. Before anything moves, I define what “done” looks like—concretely. That means no vague deliverables, no assumptions, and no room for misalignment. Every project starts with scope validation, where I break down objectives into specific, measurable tasks that tie directly to outcomes.

Actionability is the priority. A task isn’t actionable if it requires further clarification or if dependencies haven’t been addressed. I structure work into atomic units—tasks that can be started and finished without bottlenecks. This means writing clear acceptance criteria, flagging ambiguous requests early, and making sure each task contributes directly to the project’s objectives.

For documentation, I operate on a “replication-first” model—if someone else can’t pick up where we left off, the documentation isn’t good enough. Every major process, decision, and deviation gets logged. I rely on structured requirements documentation, decision registers, and retrospective notes to ensure knowledge transfer is seamless. No one should have to dig through Slack threads or email chains to understand why a decision was made.

In execution, I use progressive check-ins, not reactive problem-solving. Daily standups (if Agile), milestone reviews (if Waterfall), and real-time tracking (Wrike, Jira) ensure nothing festers. When things go off track, I focus on root causes, not symptoms—whether it’s unclear ownership, shifting priorities, or execution gaps, I correct at the source.

The goal isn’t just to complete projects—it’s to create systems that make execution repeatable, scalable, and resistant to failure.
